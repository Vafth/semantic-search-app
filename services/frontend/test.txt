The Apollo 11 mission successfully landed humans on the Moon for the first time in July 1969. Neil Armstrong became the first person to walk on the lunar surface, famously describing the moment as a giant leap for mankind. Buzz Aldrin joined him shortly after, while Michael Collins remained in orbit aboard the command module.

The Moon is approximately 384,000 kilometers from Earth. Its surface is covered in craters, dust, and ancient volcanic rock. There is no atmosphere, which means no wind, no weather, and no sound. Temperatures swing dramatically between 127 degrees Celsius in sunlight and minus 173 degrees in darkness.

Returning to space became a national priority after the Columbia Space Shuttle disaster in 2003, which killed all seven crew members during re-entry. NASA spent years redesigning safety protocols and inspection procedures before resuming crewed flights.

Mars is the next major target for human exploration. The planet has a thin atmosphere of mostly carbon dioxide and experiences massive dust storms that can engulf the entire planet for months. NASA's Perseverance rover has been collecting rock samples since 2021, searching for signs of ancient microbial life.

SpaceX, founded by Elon Musk in 2002, disrupted the space industry by developing reusable rocket technology. The Falcon 9 booster can land itself back on a drone ship after launch, dramatically reducing the cost of reaching orbit. This made commercial spaceflight economically viable for the first time.

Astronauts aboard the International Space Station experience weightlessness because they are in continuous freefall around Earth. They must exercise for two hours every day to prevent muscle and bone loss. A typical mission lasts six months, though some astronauts have stayed for over a year.
